What Did You Gain?

June 10, 2026

I cannot point to a day when I realized I was bisexual. That would mean there was a before and an after. There was not. I just always knew. I noticed the way boys looked at me. I noticed the way I looked back. I also noticed girls. The same flutter in my chest. The same curiosity. The same quiet feeling that something was different about me, even when I did not have the words for it.

I was six years old when I learned that different could be dangerous. That was also the year the sexual assault started. I will not describe it here. Not because I have forgotten. I have spent years trying to forget, and I refuse to give that memory more space than it already takes up in my head. But I will say this. It taught me that my body was not safe. That people could take things from me without asking. That silence was not a choice I made. It was how I survived.

I became closed off. Not cold. Just careful. I stopped trusting easily. I stopped reaching out. I became antisocial not because I enjoyed being alone, but because being alone was the only time I felt like I was in control. No one could hurt me if no one could get close enough. That logic saved me. It also trapped me.

By grade eight, I had built walls. Thick ones. But there was one person I let in. My best friend. We told each other everything. That was our thing. No judgment. No secrets. We would stay up late talking about nothing and everything. He knew about my bad days. I knew about his. We were young, but we understood that life was heavy in ways that adults did not always see.

So when I finally said the words out loud, I did not hesitate. I told him I thought I liked boys and girls. I trusted him. That is what best friends are supposed to do. He nodded. He said it was fine. He said he still loved me as a friend. I believed him.

For a few weeks, nothing changed. Then everything did.

I started noticing small things. People looking at me differently. Whispers that stopped when I walked into a room. Laughter that felt pointed, even when I could not hear the words. Then someone told me what was happening. He had been telling people. Not because he wanted to help me. Not because he thought I needed support. He told them because he thought it was funny. He told them because watching me panic, watching me try to control a story that was never supposed to leave the two of us, was entertaining to him.

I confronted him. He denied it. Then he admitted it. Then he laughed. "It is not that deep," he said. I have thought about that sentence for years. It is not that deep. He took something I gave him in private. Something fragile. Something I had never said to anyone else. And he turned it into a weapon. A joke. A piece of gossip to make himself more interesting.

What did he gain from that? I still do not understand. Attention, maybe. A few laughs. A reputation as someone who knew everybody's secrets. But I know what I lost.

I lost friends. People I had known for years suddenly had an excuse to distance themselves. Some were open about it. They said being around me made them uncomfortable. Others just faded away. No explanation. Just silence. I stopped talking to almost everyone. I stopped eating properly. I stopped sleeping. I stopped showing up to things I used to love. My grades dropped. My parents noticed something was wrong, but I could not tell them. I still have not told them.

The stress of hiding became a second full time job. At school, I had to manage what people knew. At home, I had to pretend everything was fine. There was no space where I could just be myself. Not fully. Not safely.

I thought about suicide more times than I can count. Not because I wanted to die. Because I was tired. Tired of pretending. Tired of being afraid. Tired of loving a world that did not seem to want me in it. I would lie in bed at night and replay everything. What if I had never told him? What if I had been more careful? What if I had just kept my mouth shut like I always did before?

The worst part was not the betrayal itself. The worst part was how it confirmed every fear I had ever had about being truly known. I spent years believing that if people really saw me, they would leave. And then someone I trusted proved me right. That broke something inside me. I am still not sure it has fully healed.

I am older now. I have built things. MountainView Group exists because I decided to stop waiting for permission to matter. I study accounting. I write code. I am becoming someone that the fourteen year old version of me would have been proud of. But I still ask the same question.

What did you gain? What did you gain from using my sexuality to embarrass me behind my back? What did you gain from telling people who had no right to know? What did you gain from making me afraid of my own truth? Was it worth it? Was the attention worth losing someone who trusted you completely?

I do not expect an answer. I stopped expecting anything from you years ago. But I need you to know what you lost.

You lost me. Not the version of me that laughed at your jokes and agreed with your opinions. The real me. The one who stayed up late listening to your problems. The one who defended you when other people talked behind your back. The one who believed that friendship meant something sacred. You will never get that back. I made sure of it.

And here is what I also know now. The people who stayed? The ones who reached out after the dust settled? They are still here. They saw what happened. They did not laugh. They did not whisper. They just showed up. Those are my people now.

If I could go back to grade eight, I would tell myself a few things. First, it is not your fault. You trusted someone. That is not weakness. That is courage. The betrayal belongs to him, not to you. Second, you are not too much. You are not broken because you feel deeply. You are not wrong because you love differently. The people who made you feel small were projecting their own discomfort. That is their work to do, not yours. Third, you will survive this. I know that sounds like something people say to make you feel better. I would have rolled my eyes at it too. But it is true. The nights that felt endless eventually ended. The pain that felt permanent eventually softened. You will carry scars. But you will also carry wisdom. Fourth, you do not owe anyone an apology for existing. Not for your sexuality. Not for your trauma. Not for the way you process the world. You are allowed to take up space. You are allowed to be complicated. You are allowed to be healing and still hurting at the same time.

I am still bisexual. That never changed. It never needed to. I am still processing. Some wounds take longer than others. That is not a failure. That is being human. I am still building. MountainView Group. Love, Nova. My understanding of finance and code. Every project I start is a small act of defiance. A way of saying I am still here. I still matter. I still have something to offer. I am still learning to trust. Slowly. Carefully. But I have not closed the door completely. That is its own kind of victory.

I started this piece with a question. What did you gain? But I realize now that I have been asking the wrong question all along. The better question is what have I gained?

I have gained clarity about who deserves my trust. I have gained strength from surviving something that should have broken me. I have gained purpose, the kind that comes from knowing what pain feels like and choosing to build instead of destroy. I have gained my own voice. Not the one I used to perform for other people's comfort. The real one. The one that can say I am bisexual without flinching. I have gained peace. Not every day. But more days than before. And I have gained permission to be exactly who I am, without apology, without explanation, without shame.

That is not nothing. That is everything.

If you are going through something similar, let me tell you what I needed to hear at fourteen. You are not wrong for being who you are. You are not weak for trusting someone. You are not broken because they broke your trust. The pain you feel right now will not last forever. I know it feels like it will. I know the nights are long and the mornings are heavier. But you will wake up one day and realize you made it through. You will find your people. They exist. They are looking for you too. And one day, you will write your own version of this. Not because the pain is gone, but because you have grown bigger than it.

I do not know if my former best friend will ever read this. I do not know if he would recognize himself in these words. I do not know if he has changed, or if he is still the same person who thought my pain was entertaining. It does not matter anymore. I am not writing this for him. I am writing this for me. For the version of me who had no one to tell. For the version of me who almost gave up. For the version of me who is still here, still standing, still speaking.

I am bisexual. I am a survivor. I am a founder. I am a student. I am a front end developer. I am all of these things at once, and none of them cancel each other out.

If you made it this far, thank you for listening. This was not easy to write. But it was necessary.

And now, I let it go.

— Nova
