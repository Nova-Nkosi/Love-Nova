The Real World Does Not Care About Your Feelings

June 22, 2026

We were sold a lie.

We were told that if we worked hard, we would succeed. That if we stayed out of trouble, we would be safe. That if we followed the rules, the rules would protect us. That the system was designed to reward merit. That the world was fair. That justice was inevitable. That being a good person was enough.

I believed it once. I was young. I did not know any better. I did not know that the world is not a meritocracy. That effort does not always translate to reward. That virtue does not guarantee safety. That the system does not care about you. That justice is not a guarantee. That being good does not protect you from being hurt.

The real world is not kind. It does not care about your feelings. It does not care about your trauma. It does not care about your intentions. It does not care about whether you meant well or whether you tried your best. It does not care about the nights you stayed up worrying. It does not care about the sacrifices you made. It does not care about the pain you carry.

The real world cares about results. It cares about leverage. It cares about power. It cares about capital. It cares about who you know and what you can offer and how much value you can generate. Everything else is noise.

That sounds harsh. I know. But I am not saying this to be cruel. I am saying it because someone should have told me this when I was younger. Someone should have told me that hard work is not enough. That being a good person does not protect you from being exploited. That playing by the rules does not mean the rules will play fair with you.

I learned this the hard way. I learned it watching people who worked twice as hard as everyone else get half as far. I learned it watching people who played by the rules get crushed by those who did not. I learned it watching the people in charge choose convenience over justice again and again. I learned it watching good people get destroyed by systems that were never designed to protect them.

And I learned it in my own life. I learned it when I trusted someone I should not have trusted. I learned it when I believed that honesty would be reciprocated. I learned it when I discovered that the world does not reward vulnerability. It exploits it.

This is not cynicism. This is clarity. This is the difference between what I was told and what I have lived. This is the moment when you realize that the fairy tale was never meant to protect you. It was meant to keep you quiet. It was meant to keep you compliant. It was meant to keep you believing that if you just tried a little harder, everything would work out.

The world does not work that way.

The world is full of people who will take advantage of your trust. Who will exploit your kindness. Who will weaponize your vulnerability. Who will use your story against you. Who will smile at you while they plan your downfall. The world is full of people who will tell you what you want to hear and then do the opposite. People who will promise you the world and give you nothing.

And yet.

And yet I am still here. And yet I am still building. And yet I am still choosing to believe that something else is possible. Not because I am naive. Not because I have not seen what the world is capable of. But because I refuse to let the world make me small.

I refuse to become the person who says: this is just how it is. I refuse to become the person who accepts injustice as inevitable. I refuse to become the person who turns away from pain because it is easier than looking. I refuse to become the person who gave up.

That is not optimism. That is resistance. That is a choice. A choice to keep going even when the world gives you every reason to stop. A choice to keep building even when everything you build gets knocked down. A choice to keep trusting even when trust has been weaponized against you.

The real world does not care about your feelings. That is true. But that does not mean you should stop feeling. It means you should feel deeply and act wisely. It means you should carry your pain without letting it define you. It means you should see the world for what it is and still choose to be kind.

That is the hardest thing. To know how cruel the world can be and still refuse to become cruel yourself. To see how selfish people can be and still choose to be generous. To watch the system crush people you love and still believe that change is possible.

I have been hurt. I have been betrayed. I have been used. I have been dismissed. I have been told that I am not enough, that I will never be enough, that I should lower my expectations and accept the crumbs I am given. And I have refused. Every single time.

Not because I am special. But because I have seen what happens when people stop refusing. When they accept the lie. When they decide that resistance is futile. When they let the world make them small. That is the real tragedy. Not the pain. The surrender.

So here is what I know.

The real world is hard. It is unfair. It will break your heart. It will disappoint you. It will test everything you believe. It will ask you to compromise. It will ask you to settle. It will ask you to accept less than you deserve.

But the real world is also where you get to choose. Every day. Every moment. You get to choose whether you will be bitter or brave. Whether you will be cynical or kind. Whether you will give up or keep going.

I choose to keep going.

Not because I think the world will change overnight. It will not. But because I believe that every small act of resistance matters. Every time you refuse to look away. Every time you refuse to accept the unacceptable. Every time you tell the truth when it would be easier to lie. Every time you choose to be kind when the world gives you every reason to be cruel.

That is how the world changes. Not all at once. But one refusal at a time.

So I keep going. I keep building. I keep writing. I keep speaking. I keep refusing to accept that this is all there is.

The real world does not care about your feelings.

But I do. And that has to count for something.

— Nova
