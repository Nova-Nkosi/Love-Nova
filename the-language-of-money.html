The Language of Money

March 20, 2026

I study accounting. I also write code. People ask me why.

They see two different worlds. One is about rules, frameworks, and financial statements. The other is about logic, structure, and telling machines what to do. On the surface, they could not be more different. One deals with the past, recording what has already happened. The other deals with the future, building what could be.

But to me, they are the same thing.

Money is a language. It flows through systems the way data flows through code. Banks, transactions, interest rates, asset valuations. All of it runs on invisible rules. If you understand those rules, you can build things. If you don't, you just watch from the outside. You become a passenger in a system that moves without your input.

I don't want to just watch.

I want to understand the architecture. I want to see the scaffolding beneath the surface. I want to know why money moves the way it does, why some people accumulate wealth while others struggle, why certain policies create opportunity and others create barriers. These are not just economic questions. They are structural questions. And structures can be redesigned.

That is what draws me to financial engineering. It is not a fancy term for something abstract. It is the practice of using tools, mathematical, computational, and strategic, to solve financial problems. It is about building models that predict behavior. It is about designing systems that are more efficient, more transparent, more fair. It is about using code to understand capital, and using capital to build something that outlasts you.

That is why I started MountainView Group. Not because I had everything figured out. I did not. I still don't. But I knew that waiting for permission to matter was not an option. So I built something. Something small at first. Something that helps small businesses keep their books straight. Something that makes accounting education free for students who cannot afford expensive tutors. Something that treats financial literacy not as a privilege, but as a right.

MountainView Accounting handles the books. MountainView Education handles the knowledge. MountainView Group holds it all together. It is not an empire. It is a beginning. But every empire starts with a single brick.

And that is also why I am learning to code. Not because I want to be a programmer in the traditional sense. But because I believe that the people who understand both finance and technology will shape the next decade. They will build the tools that democratize access to capital. They will create platforms that make financial systems more accountable. They will write the algorithms that decide who gets funded, who gets hired, who gets a second chance.

I want to be one of them.

There is a misconception that accounting is about counting beans. That it is dry. That it is backward-looking. But accounting is not just about recording the past. It is about telling the truth. It is about creating a narrative that is honest, verifiable, and transparent. It is about saying: this is what happened, and here is the evidence.

Coding is the same thing. It is about creating systems that are honest. That do what they are supposed to do. That do not hide their logic behind a black box. That are transparent, verifiable, and accountable. Good code is like good accounting. It tells the truth.

So yes, I study debits and credits. And yes, I write JavaScript. It feels strange to some people. They think I am trying to be two things at once. But I am not trying to be two things. I am trying to be one thing: someone who understands how systems work, and who is not afraid to build new ones.

There is a word for this. It is called financial engineering. But I prefer to think of it as building bridges. Between finance and technology. Between theory and practice. Between what is and what could be.

Money is not just currency. It is energy. It flows through economies the way electricity flows through circuits. It can be generated. It can be stored. It can be transferred. It can be wasted. And like electricity, it can be harnessed to do remarkable things, or it can be left to dissipate into nothing.

I want to learn how to channel that energy. Not for personal wealth alone. That would be too small a goal. But for something larger. For access. For opportunity. For a world where financial literacy is not a luxury, but a birthright. Where young people from townships and villages and struggling suburbs understand how money works, not because they are exceptional, but because the system was designed to teach them.

That is the world I want to build.

It will not happen overnight. It will not happen by accident. It will happen because people like me, people who see the connection between code and capital, decide to build it. One line of code at a time. One balance sheet at a time. One student at a time.

I am not there yet. But I am on my way.

And every day, I am learning to speak the language of money a little more fluently.

— Nova
