The NSFAS Living Allowance Debate

June 14, 2026

There has been a lot of noise online about the NSFAS living allowance. R1700 a month. People call it peanuts. And honestly, I get it. It is not a lot of money. It was never meant to be a lot of money. It is meant to be pocket money for students who already have their major expenses covered.

But here is what the online debates always leave out.

NSFAS does not just give you R1700. They pay for your full tuition. Every cent. You do not see a bill. You do not have to beg family for registration fees. It is covered. They also pay up to R54 000 for accommodation. That is rent. Paid. You do not have to worry about where you will sleep while you study. And they give you a textbook allowance of R5678. Your books. Your materials. Taken care of.

So when people say R1700 is not enough, what exactly are they spending it on? Tuition? Covered. Accommodation? Covered. Textbooks? Covered. The R1700 is for transport, data, food, and personal essentials. That is it. That is what a living allowance is for. It is not supposed to cover your lifestyle. It is supposed to keep you alive while you focus on your studies.

Now, before anyone calls me classist or privileged, let me stop you right there. I am a student. I am not rich. I come from a home where money does not grow on trees. I have had to count every cent, stretch every rand, and make decisions between food and transport more times than I care to remember. I am not speaking from a place of privilege. I am speaking from a place of experience.

R1700 is not comfortable. It is not luxurious. It will not buy you new clothes or takeout every night or a nice apartment close to campus. But if you are careful, if you plan, if you prioritize, it can cover the basics. Rice. Bread. Eggs. Transport to and from class. Data for assignments. It is survival money. And for many of us, survival money is the difference between staying in university and dropping out.

What frustrates me is that when we try to have an honest conversation about budgeting and financial discipline, we are accused of defending the system. I am not defending NSFAS. I am not saying the living allowance should not be increased. Of course it should. Everything is more expensive now. Transport costs have gone up. Food prices have gone up. Data is still expensive. R1700 today does not go as far as it did five years ago. That is a fact.

But calling people classist for pointing out that you can survive on R1700 when your tuition, rent, and books are already paid for? That helps no one. It shuts down conversation. It turns potential allies into enemies. It makes it impossible to talk about what actually needs to change.

Here is the truth. There are students who have no family support at all. There are students who are raising siblings while studying. There are students who have medical conditions that require expensive treatment. For those students, R1700 is absolutely not enough. And we should be fighting for them. We should be demanding more. We should be asking why a country with so much wealth cannot take better care of its future leaders.

But there are also students who waste the money. Who spend it on things they do not need. Who do not budget. Who do not plan. Who expect the government to fund a lifestyle instead of an education. And when those students call the rest of us classist for saying something uncomfortable, it makes the whole movement look unserious.

Let me break it down. Tuition? Paid. Accommodation? Up to R54 000 paid. Textbooks? R5678 paid. That means your university education is essentially free. The only thing you need to figure out is how to eat and get to class. Yes, R1700 is tight. Yes, it should be increased. But if you cannot survive on R1700 for transport and food when everything else is covered, the problem might not just be the allowance.

I am not saying you should be grateful for scraps. I am saying that if you have R1700 and you want to stay in university, you will learn very quickly what matters and what does not. You will learn that a nice phone is not a necessity. That eating out is a luxury. That you can walk instead of taking an Uber. That you can buy in bulk and cook at home. These are not insults. These are survival skills. And they are skills that will serve you long after you graduate, no matter how much money you eventually make.

So here is what I believe. We can walk and chew gum at the same time. We can advocate for a higher living allowance while also encouraging financial literacy. We can fight for systemic change while also taking personal responsibility for the choices we make. These things are not opposites. They are partners.

Calling someone classist for telling you to budget does not put more food on your table. It does not lower your transport costs. It does not increase your allowance. All it does is make you feel better about choices you know you could have made differently. And I say that with love, because I have been there too. I have wasted money I should not have wasted. I have made decisions I regret. I am not pointing fingers. I am just asking us to be honest.

NSFAS needs to do better. The government needs to do better. But we also need to do better with what we have while we fight for more. That is not classist. That is just reality.

And if that makes some people angry, so be it. I would rather tell the truth and be called names than pretend that R1700 is nothing when for some students it is literally the only thing standing between them and dropping out. But let us also not pretend that R1700 is the only thing NSFAS gives. Tuition. Accommodation. Textbooks. All covered.

R1700 is peanuts. But peanuts can keep you alive while you fight for the meal. Especially when someone else is already paying for the table, the chair, and the plate.

— Nova
