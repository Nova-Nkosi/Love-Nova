The Weight of Knowing

June 20, 2026

I was young when I first realized that the world was not fair.

Not in the way children learn that some people have more toys than others. That is surface level. That is easy to dismiss. No, I learned it in the way that settles into your bones. The way that makes you look at adults differently. The way that makes you realize that the people in charge are not always right, and that the systems we trust are not always designed to protect us.

I was young when I first understood that corruption was not just a word in a textbook. It was real. It was happening around me. It was the reason some people had running water and others did not. It was the reason some schools had libraries and others had holes in the roof. It was the reason some families could afford medical care and others had to pray.

Once you see it, you cannot unsee it.

That is the weight of knowing. It is the burden of awareness. It is the feeling of being awake in a room full of sleeping people, and realizing that no one is coming to wake them up. You have to do it yourself.

I remember sitting in class, listening to a teacher explain how democracy works, and thinking: but that is not how it actually works. I remember watching the news and seeing politicians promise things they never delivered. I remember hearing adults talk about "the system" as if it was something abstract, something that just existed, something that could not be changed. And I remember thinking: but someone built this system. Someone designed it. And if someone designed it, someone else can redesign it.

That thought never left me.

It followed me into high school. It followed me into university. It follows me still. Every time I open a textbook, every time I look at a financial statement, every time I write a line of code, I am asking the same question: who does this system serve? And who does it leave behind?

The answer is rarely comforting.

The weight of knowing is heavy because it demands something from you. It does not let you sit back and watch. It does not let you pretend that everything is fine. It does not let you say "that is just how things are." Once you know, you are responsible. Not for fixing everything. That is impossible. But for doing something. Anything. For not looking away.

I have felt that weight in my chest since I was a teenager. I have felt it when I walk through my neighborhood and see the gaps between what should be and what is. I have felt it when I talk to other young people who are exhausted by the same things I am exhausted by. I have felt it when I read the news and realize that nothing has changed.

But I have also felt something else. Something that is not quite hope, but is close. A quiet refusal to accept that this is all there is. A stubborn belief that the world can be different because it has been different before. A certainty that the people who built the systems we live in were not gods. They were just people. People with power. People with decisions. People who could be replaced by other people with better decisions.

That is what keeps me going. Not optimism. That feels too naive. But a kind of stubborn determination. A refusal to let the weight crush me.

The weight of knowing is not a burden you carry alone. It is shared by everyone who has ever looked at the world and said: this is not good enough. This is not fair. This is not what we were promised.

We carry it together. We hold it for each other. We remind each other that knowing is not the end of the story. It is the beginning.

It takes courage to know. It takes more courage to act. And it takes the most courage to keep acting even when nothing seems to change.

I am still learning how to carry this weight without being crushed by it. Some days I fail. Some days I close my eyes and pretend I do not see. Some days I want to be ignorant. I want to be the person who does not know, who does not care, who just lives their life and does not think about the rest.

But I cannot. The knowing is already there. It is part of me now.

So I keep going. I study. I build. I write. I speak. I try to be the person I needed when I was younger and first realized that the world was not fair. The person who did not look away. The person who said: I see this. And I refuse to accept it.

That is the weight of knowing. It is heavy. But it is also sacred.

And I will carry it for as long as I have to.

— Nova
